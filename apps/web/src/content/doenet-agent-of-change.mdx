Last week, I needed to re-grout my shower. Textbooks, learning and problem-solving have changed quite a bit in my lifetime. I no longer read about how to work on my shower. Instead, I go online and watch a YouTube video. There is interaction between my task and the feedback I receive while I am in the messy process of trying to figure out what I am doing.

The same is happening in my classroom. Students no longer read textbooks, they can get their lecture information from short videos. Students are quite capable of following recipes to calculate answers, but so are machines. The human endeavor is no longer the computation, but the communication of ideas and the creativity to make connections among concepts.

Doenet allows my students to explore mathematical concepts in ways that I was not able to do in the classroom a decade ago. My greatest joy is when students tell me ‘I used to hate math, but this course is fun.’ Not only is it fun, but we are seeing positive results as well.

Data was collected from nine Big Ten schools in large first-year STEM programs (PreCalculus, Calculus I and Chemistry I).

Of importance is the DFW rate, also known as the ‘failure rate’, the rate at which students get a grade below ‘C-’ (either a D or F) or withdraw from the course. The DFW rate for the majority population and for UnderRepresented Minorities (URM) were computed (see [Figure 1](#figure-1)). Most of the bars show a wide spread. In the bottom left is the Univ. of Minnesota PreCalculus program. Two things are noticeable:

1. The DFW rate is low. Some may argue that maybe the instructors are just easy graders. Fair enough. I’ll address this in a moment.
2. The ratio of the DFW rates is low. (see [Figure 2](#figure-2)).

URM students are only 25% more likely to fail than the majority population. This
is the best number of the 25 programs studied. We are Big Ten Champs!

Another success story is what happens after PreCalculus. I haven’t done my job as a PreCalculus instructor unless PreCalculus prepares students for Calculus. Students that go through our program outperform students who had a different preparation for college-level Calc I, including students that took AP Calculus in high school.

The key is involving students in problem-solving and mathematical communication in the classroom and Doenet is the ideal tool for this purpose.
